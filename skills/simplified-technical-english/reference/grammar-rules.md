# ASD-STE100 Writing Rules (Part 1)

Source: ASD-STE100 Issue 7, 2017-01-25. 53 rules across 9 sections.

## Section 1 - Words

### Rule 1.1 — You can use words that are approved in the dictionary, technical names, or technical verbs.

**Example:** Only these three categories of words are permitted in STE text.

### Rule 1.2 — Use approved words from the dictionary only as the part of speech given.

**Example:** "Work" is approved as a noun, not a verb → "Do work" not "Work with..."

### Rule 1.3 — Use approved words only with their approved meanings.

**Example:** "Above"/"below" apply to physical position only, not to limits.

### Rule 1.4 — Use only the approved forms of verbs and adjectives.

**Example:** Use the verb forms (infinitive, imperative, present, past, past participle) listed in the dictionary entry.

### Rule 1.5 — You can use words that you can include in a technical name category (19 categories, e.g. parts, tools, materials, documents).

**Example:** "Actuator", "flange", "circuit breaker" are technical names.

### Rule 1.6 — Use a word that is unapproved in the dictionary only when it is a technical name or part of a technical name.

**Example:** "Main" is unapproved (use "primary"), but "main landing gear" is a technical name and is kept as-is.

### Rule 1.7 — Do not use words that are technical names as verbs.

**Example:** Non-STE: _Oil the steel surfaces._ → STE: _Apply oil to the steel surfaces._

### Rule 1.8 — Use technical names that agree with approved nomenclature.

**Example:** Use the official parts-data name (e.g. "touchscreen") rather than an invented equivalent.

### Rule 1.9 — When you must select a technical name, use one which is short and easy to understand.

**Example:** Non-STE: _stainless steel pan head machine screws_ → STE: _screws_ (when the illustration clarifies it).

### Rule 1.10 — Do not use slang or jargon words as technical names.

**Example:** Non-STE: _foot-long breaker bar_ → STE: _12-inch wrench handle_.

### Rule 1.11 — Do not use different technical names for the same item.

**Example:** Pick one term (e.g. "actuator") and use it consistently instead of switching between "actuator", "servo control unit", "control unit".

### Rule 1.12 — You can use verbs that you can include in a technical verb category (4 categories: manufacturing processes, computer processes/applications, descriptions, operational language).

**Example:** "Ream", "click", "modulate", "taxi" are technical verbs in their respective categories.

### Rule 1.13 — Do not use technical verbs as nouns.

**Example:** Non-STE: _Give the hole 0.20-inch over-ream._ → STE: _Ream the hole 0.20 inch larger than the standard._ (Past participles of technical verbs, e.g. "reamed", can be used as adjectives.)

### Rule 1.14 — Use American English spelling.

**Example:** "Fiber" not "fibre"; "color" not "colour".

## Section 2 - Noun clusters

### Rule 2.1 — Write noun clusters of no more than three words.

Long strings of nouns/adjectives modifying a head noun are hard to parse, especially for non-native readers, because relations between the words are ambiguous. Use prepositions ("of", "on", "for") to break up long clusters. Articles and prepositions don't count toward the three-word limit.
**Example:** Non-STE: _Runway light connection resistance calibration._ → STE: _Calibration of the resistance of the runway light connection._

### Rule 2.2 — When a technical name has more than three words, write it in full. Then simplify it by giving a shorter name, or by using hyphens between words used as a single unit.

Write the long official name in full the first time, then use a shorter name/abbreviation afterward. Hyphens show which words function as one unit, but do not hyphenate more than three words together.
**Example:** Full first use: _Main Fuel Metering Unit (MFMU)_ → shorter afterward: _MFMU_. Hyphen: _the landing-light cutoff-switch power connection_.

### Rule 2.3 — When applicable, use an article (the, a, an) or a demonstrative adjective (this, these) before a noun.

Do not omit articles to save words — it causes ambiguity. Do not use articles in general statements. Do not put a definite article before a noun followed by an alphanumeric identifier.
**Example:** Non-STE: _Turn shaft assembly._ → STE: _Turn the shaft assembly._ Also: _Tag circuit breaker 36L7_ (not "the circuit breaker 36L7").

## Section 3 - Verbs

### Rule 3.1 — Use only those forms of the verb that are given in the dictionary.

**Example:** The STE dictionary lists infinitive/imperative, simple present, simple past, and past participle (as adjective) forms for each approved verb.

### Rule 3.2 — Use the approved forms of the verb to make only: the infinitive, the imperative, the simple present tense, the simple past tense, the past participle (as an adjective), and the future tense.

No other tenses (e.g. present perfect, continuous) are permitted.

### Rule 3.3 — Use the past participle only as an adjective.

Use it before a noun, or after "to be"/"to become", to describe a condition — this is not passive voice. Irregular participles like "been" are never used as adjectives.
**Example:** _Connect the disconnected wires._ / _The wires are disconnected._

### Rule 3.4 — Do not use helping verbs to make complex verb structures.

Do not combine the past participle with "have" (present perfect) or with modal/auxiliary verbs ("can", "must", "will", "to be") to form complex passive sentences.
**Example:** Non-STE: _The temperature must be adjusted._ → STE: _Adjust the temperature._

### Rule 3.5 — Use the "-ing" form of a verb only as a modifier in a technical name.

"-ing" words used as verbs, adjectives, nouns, or in dependent clauses cause ambiguity and are generally not permitted. As a technical-name modifier (e.g. "grinding wheel") it is fine.
**Example:** Non-STE: _When you are doing this procedure, obey all the safety precautions._ → STE: _When you do this procedure, obey all the safety precautions._

### Rule 3.6 — Use only the active voice in procedural writing. Use the active voice as much as possible in descriptive writing.

Test for passive voice by asking "by whom or by what?" Four methods to convert passive to active: (1) put the agent at the start as subject, (2) change an infinitive verb to an active verb, (3) in procedures, use the imperative, (4) use "you" or "we" as subject when no agent is given.
**Example:** Non-STE: _The main gear leg is held by the side stay._ → STE: _The side stay holds the main gear leg._

### Rule 3.7 — Use an approved verb to describe an action (not a noun or other parts of speech).

Nouns describe actions abstractly; verbs show them directly.
**Example:** Non-STE: _The ohmmeter gives an indication of 450 ± 22 ohms._ → STE: _The ohmmeter shows 450 ± 22 ohms._

## Section 4 - Sentences

### Rule 4.1 — Write short and clear sentences.

Give specific information; address the reader directly; avoid vague/abstract statements.
**Example:** Non-STE: _No leaks permitted._ → STE: _Make sure that there are no leaks._

### Rule 4.2 — Do not omit words or use contractions to make your sentences shorter.

Do not omit nouns, verbs, subjects, articles, or parts of contractions ("don't", "isn't"). Omissions save words but hurt clarity, especially in safety instructions.
**Example:** Non-STE: _If installed, remove the shims._ → STE: _If shims are installed, remove them._

### Rule 4.3 — Use a vertical list for complex texts.

Put a colon before the list; identify each item with a number/letter/dash/bullet; capitalize each item's first letter; punctuate full-sentence items with a period, otherwise no end punctuation except the last item gets a period. Do not hide negative information — repeat negative commands in each list item if needed.
**Example:** STE: _The wheel assembly has these parts: - The tire - The tube - The spokes..._

### Rule 4.4 — Use connecting words and phrases to connect sentences that contain related topics.

Approved connectors include "and", "but", "then", "thus", "as a result"; pronouns and demonstrative adjectives ("this", "they") can also connect ideas.
**Example:** _The localizer course is aligned with the centerline of the runway. And the glideslope path is at a constant angle to the threshold._

## Section 5 - Procedural writing

### Rule 5.1 — Write short sentences. Use a maximum of 20 words in each sentence.

Applies to warnings, cautions, and other safety instructions too.

### Rule 5.2 — Write only one instruction in each sentence unless two or more actions occur at the same time.

Use numbered/lettered steps for sequence; combine only when actions are simultaneous or a result occurs immediately after an action.
**Example:** STE: _(1) De-energize the system. (2) Make sure that all switches on the control panel are in their correct position._

### Rule 5.3 — Write instructions in the imperative (command) form.

Avoids ambiguity about whether a step is optional, already done, or for someone else to do later.
**Example:** Non-STE: _The test can be continued._ → STE: _Continue the test._

### Rule 5.4 — If you start an instruction with a descriptive statement (dependent phrase or clause), divide that statement from the command with a comma.

The comma placement can change meaning — be careful.
**Example:** _When the light comes on, set the switch to NORMAL._

### Rule 5.5 — Write notes only to give information, not instructions.

Notes must not contain the imperative form or safety-critical information (that belongs in a warning/caution). Notes have a maximum of 25 words and are for procedures only (not general descriptions), unless needed for illustrations/tables.
**Example:** NOTE: _The gyroscope will become stable after approximately 15 seconds._

## Section 6 - Descriptive writing

### Rule 6.1 — Give information gradually.

Each sentence should contain only one topic; build up the picture step by step rather than dumping information at once.

### Rule 6.2 — Use key words and phrases to organize your text logically.

Repeating key words/phrases across sentences (plus connecting words like "and", "but", "thus", "as a result") links ideas and gives the text logical structure.

### Rule 6.3 — Write short sentences. Use a maximum of 25 words in each sentence.

Descriptive text is more complex than procedural text, so the limit is higher than in Section 5 (20 words).

### Rule 6.4 — Use paragraphs to show related information.

Start each paragraph with a topic sentence that states its subject; subsequent sentences explain or support it. A new paragraph signals new/different information.

### Rule 6.5 — Make sure that each paragraph has only one topic.

The topic sentence is the first and most important sentence; readers should be able to build a good outline by reading only topic sentences.

### Rule 6.6 — Make sure that no paragraph has more than six sentences.

Long paragraphs stop holding the reader's attention; split into smaller paragraphs if needed.

## Section 7 - Safety instructions

**Definitions:** A **warning** tells the reader there is a risk of injury or death to persons. A **caution** tells the reader there is a risk of damage to objects/equipment. (Other industries may use "danger", "attention", "notice", or symbols — same principles apply; see ANSI Z535.6 and ISO 3864-2.)

### Rule 7.1 — Use an applicable word (for example, "warning" or "caution") to identify the level of risk.

Do an accurate risk analysis to decide whether the hazard is injury/death (warning) or equipment damage (caution), or both.
**Example:** WARNING: _BEFORE YOU FILL THE LIQUID OXYGEN SYSTEM, PUT ON PROTECTIVE CLOTHING. LIQUID OXYGEN IS DANGEROUS._

### Rule 7.2 — Start a safety instruction with a clear and simple command or condition.

State the command, or the condition the reader must know first, right at the start.
**Example:** WARNING: _DO NOT SWALLOW THE SOLVENT. ... SOLVENTS ARE POISONOUS AND CAN CAUSE INJURY OR DEATH TO PERSONNEL._

### Rule 7.3 — Give an explanation to show the specific risk or possible result.

Tell the reader exactly what will happen if the instruction is not obeyed, so they understand the risk.
**Example:** CAUTION: _WHEN YOU ASSEMBLE THE UNIT, DO NOT LET THE PARTS FALL. IF THEY FALL, PERMANENT DAMAGE CAN OCCUR._

## Section 8 - Punctuation and word counts

### Rule 8.1 — You can use all standard English punctuation marks except the semicolon (;).

Semicolons permit overly long sentences and are hard to use correctly; write two separate sentences instead.

### Rule 8.2 — Use hyphens (-) to connect closely related words.

Applies to: multi-word adjectives before a noun (_low-altitude flight_), two-word fractions/numbers (_forty-seven_), capital-letter/number + noun shape terms (_L-shaped bracket_, _3-prong connector_), verbs with a noun/other part of speech as first element (_die-cast_, _air-condition_), and prefix-ending-in-vowel + root-starting-with-vowel (_de-ice_, _pre-engage_).

### Rule 8.3 — You can use parentheses to: make references to illustrations/text; include letters/numbers identifying items; identify steps in a procedure; include abbreviations; give singular/plural forms at once; explain words or part of a sentence; include an alternative.

**Example:** _Remove the valve (10, Figure 1)._ / _Install the component(s) before you do the test(s)._ / _Open the left (right) access panel L42 (R42)._

### Rule 8.4 — In a vertical list, a colon (:) has the same effect on word count as a full stop (period) and shows the end of a sentence.

Max 20 words before the colon in procedural sentences, 25 in descriptive sentences; each list item after the colon counts as its own new sentence subject to the same limits.

### Rule 8.5 — When you put text in parentheses, it counts as one word in that sentence.

The parenthetical content itself is a separate sentence with its own word count. Identifiers/abbreviations in parentheses also count as one word.
**Example:** _Remove the safety pin (10)._ (5 words)

### Rule 8.6 — Count each of these as one word: numbers, units of measurement, abbreviations, alphanumeric identifiers, quoted text, titles/headings/placards.

**Example:** _The unit weighs 20 kg._ (4 words) / _Tag circuit breaker 36L7._ (4 words, "36L7" = 1 word).

### Rule 8.7 — Hyphenated words count as one word.

**Example:** _Clean the surface with a soap-and-water solution._ (7 words, "soap-and-water" = 1 word).

## Section 9 - Writing practices

### Rule 9.1 — Use a different construction to write a sentence when a word-for-word replacement is not sufficient.

This happens when: (1) the grammatical structure must change to use the replacement word, (2) a literal word-for-word swap gives a meaningless result, (3) the approved alternative changes the sentence's meaning, or (4) the word to replace isn't in the dictionary at all. In all cases, ask what the sentence really means and reconstruct it rather than forcing a substitution.
**Example:** Non-STE: _The oil level on the sight gauge must be visible during the test._ → STE: _Make sure that you can see the oil level on the sight gauge during the test._

### Rule 9.2 — Use each approved word correctly.

Approved STE words often have only one specific meaning (narrower than general English) — check the dictionary's approved meaning before use.
**Example:** Non-STE: _Wear protective clothing._ ("wear" is only an approved noun) → STE: _Use (or put on) protective clothing._

### Rule 9.3 — When you use two words together, do not make phrasal verbs.

Phrasal verbs (verb + preposition) create new, often abstract meanings distinct from their parts, causing ambiguity; use a single approved verb with the intended meaning instead.
**Example:** Non-STE: _After you put out the fire, close the valve._ → STE: _After you extinguish the fire, close the valve._

### Rule 9.4 — When you select terminology or wording, always use a consistent style.

Once you choose a name or a sentence pattern for a recurring action (e.g. removing/installing a part), reuse it identically every time the same context/meaning recurs, so the reader recognizes it instantly.

### GR-1 — The conjunction "that"

Do not omit "that" after verbs like "make sure", "show", "recommend" — it marks where the subordinate clause begins and prevents ambiguity.
**Example:** Do not write: _Make sure the valve is open._ → WRITE: _Make sure that the valve is open._

### GR-2 — The preposition "with"

"With" has three approved meanings (association/relationship, help/sharing, means/instrument) and can be ambiguous — reread sentences using "with" to confirm the intended meaning is clear.
**Example:** Do not write: _Carefully seal the opening with the specified tool._ → WRITE: _Use the specified tool to seal the opening._

### GR-3 — How to use pronouns

If a pronoun ("it", "they", "that", "these", "those") could refer to more than one preceding noun, replace it with the specific noun(s) it refers to.
**Example:** Do not write: _If you engage the pins incorrectly with the seats, they can become damaged._ → WRITE: _...the pins can become damaged_ (or "the seats", or "the pins and seats").

### GR-4 — The pronoun "this"

If "this" could refer to more than one preceding idea, restate the applicable context explicitly instead of leaving it ambiguous.
**Example:** Do not write: _Make sure that the cover is not locked (this can cause damage to the probe.)_ → WRITE: _Make sure that the cover is not locked. If the cover is locked, this can cause damage to the probe._
